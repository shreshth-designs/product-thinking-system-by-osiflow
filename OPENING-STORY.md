# Opening Story — 3 Slides
# Session 2: The Product-Minded Designer

---

## SLIDE A — The Setup

**On slide:**
> Duolingo's designers hated the streak feature.
> "It's gamey. It cheapens education."

**Say:**
"When Duolingo first built streaks — the little flame that shows how many days in a row you've practiced — the design team pushed back hard. It looked like a mobile game. It felt like manipulation. It wasn't what a serious education product should do. The designers were right, aesthetically. But they were asking the wrong question."

---

## SLIDE B — The Turn

**On slide:**
> The PM ran one number.
> Users with streaks retained 2x better.
> The North Star said: keep it.

**Say:**
"The PM pulled the data. Users who had a streak of 7 days or more were twice as likely to still be using Duolingo 30 days later. Not 10% better. Twice. The North Star metric — daily active learners — moved every time streaks moved. The design instinct said this is wrong. The outcome data said this is the product.

They kept it. They made it more prominent. They built loss aversion into it — you get a notification when you're about to lose your streak. The designers originally thought that was dark. The PM thought: this is the feature."

---

## SLIDE C — The Connect

**On slide:**
> Today: 500 million users.
> The streak is their most recognised feature.
> The designer's instinct was wrong.
> The outcome data was right.

**Say:**
"Duolingo is now one of the most used apps on earth. And the feature everyone wanted to kill is the one people talk about most. The lesson isn't 'ignore design instinct.' The lesson is: your instinct needs a question attached to it. What's the one number? Who are we designing for? What would happen if this didn't exist?

Those are PM questions. And the designers who ask them are the ones in the room when the decision gets made.

That's what today is about."
